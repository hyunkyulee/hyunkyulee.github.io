Directed Training is Effective for Individuals with Lower Fluid Intelligence

Summary

We used training strategy and individual differences in baseline fluid intelligence to predict training success in a complex video game. Fifty non-game-playing young adults were trained on a game called Space Fortress for 30 hours with one of two training regimens: 1) Hybrid Variable-Priority Training (HVT), with part-task training and a focus on improving specific skills and managing task priorities, and 2) Full Emphasis Training (FET) in which participants practiced the whole game to obtain the highest overall score. The relationship between individual differences in fluid intelligence and overall game improvement score differed as a function of training regimen. With FET, baseline fluid intelligence was positively associated with game improvement. In contrast, with HVT, baseline fluid intelligence was negatively associated with game improvement, suggesting that initial training disadvantage due to individual differences in baseline fluid intelligence can be overcome with training that emphasizes component tasks and flexible task coordination. By reanalyzing training effects in terms of individual differences, the current study offers a training approach that maximizes training gain and minimizes the potentially limiting effect of initial cognitive abilities.

Methods

Training Regimens: For the HVT group, the first 5 sessions were a combination of 70 minutes of part-task training and 50 minutes of Variable Priority Training (VPT). After the first 5 sessions, participants completed 10 sessions of VPT. For the FET group, throughout the 15 sessions, participants were always asked to maximize total score during practice. The limited-exposure control group played the Space Fortress game for only 3 sessions: at the beginning of the training, at the average time-point after which training groups had completed 10 hours of training, and at the end of the training. A complete description of each training regimen is presented in Lee et al (2012).

Results

Figure 2. Partial correlation between general inelligence and game performance across sessions while controlling gender

To determine the extent to which baseline fluid intelligence predicts game performance, we examined the correlation between baseline composite scores and the Space Fortress performance across the training sessions for each training group (Figure 2). Gender was included as a control variable. In FET, there were positive correlations between baseline fluid intelligence score and game performance across the training sessions. The correlations between session 1 and session 15 were not significantly different, t(22) = -.88, p >.05. However, in HVT, the positive correlation between baseline executive function and game performance decreased half way through training, and there was significant difference between correlations in session 1 and session 15, t(22) = 2.59, p =.016

Figure 3. Relation between general intelligence and training success

To examine if baseline fluid intelligence would predict the amount of game improvement, we computed correlations between the amount of game improvement (session 15- session1) and baseline fluid intelligence (see Figure 3). Gender and initial game scores were included as control variables. The correlation between game improvement and baseline fluid intelligence was marginally significant for FET and significant for HVT groups, however the direction of effect differed in two training groups (r = .388, p = .06 for FET, r = -.476, p = .022 for HVT). When we compared correlations between training groups using Fishers Z transformation, there was significant difference between groups, z = 3.08, p < .01. In FET, participants with higher baseline fluid intelligence tended to improve more than participants with lower baseline fluid intelligence. In contrast, in HVT, participants who started with lower fluid intelligence function improved more in the game than those who started with higher baseline fluid intelligence.

Conclusion

In sum, the findings described herein provide insight into designing cognitive training protocols that maximize training gain and minimize the influence of individual differences. Future research should focus on identifying sensitive metrics of individual differences, such as how different neural networks are associated with individual differences, training regimens, and their interaction.
